Write Prompts for Image Generation: Styles and Rules

By Evie Rao • August 12, 2025

Learning how to write prompts for image generation is like planning a photo shoot in words. You define what matters, constrain what does not, and leave just enough room for creative lift. Use the phases below to move from a vague idea to reproducible, brand-safe visuals that hold up across campaigns and tools.

These steps work across Midjourney, DALL·E, Stable Diffusion, and Adobe Firefly. The vocabulary differs, yet the underlying controls remain consistent: subjects, style, lighting, and constraints. Treat each phase as a checklist and your results will become faster, clearer, and far easier to iterate.

Define Subjects and Frame the Shot

Start by locking the subject and composition before you mention style. Models respond best when the first tokens anchor the scene: who or what, how many, doing what, and where. Express specific counts and relationships. Say “one cyclist in a yellow raincoat rides toward camera on a wet city street” rather than “cyclist in city.” Add perspective cues such as “eye level,” “low angle,” or “overhead,” and choose an aspect ratio that matches the channel. A 4:5 portrait fits Instagram feed, while 16:9 suits website heroes. The goal is to remove ambiguity so the model spends its creativity on nuance, not guessing your intent.

Translate compositional ideas into photographic language. Use “rule of thirds,” “centered symmetry,” “tight head-and-shoulders,” or “wide environmental portrait.” If you know focal lengths, write them in: “35mm street photo” for context with mild distortion, “85mm portrait” for flattering compression, or “macro 100mm” for texture. Camera shorthand reduces variance across generations because it encodes spatial expectations. Even with illustrated or 3D styles, these cues still guide framing reliably.

Turn this into a repeatable mini-brief. Write a one-sentence subject line, a one-sentence action and setting, and a one-sentence composition constraint. Keep them in that order for every prompt. This simple pattern improves consistency when collaborating across teams or tools. If you plan to attach reference images, specify their role: “use reference only for pose” or “match color palette from reference, not the background.” For a deeper framework that mixes text and reference images, see the guide to multimodal prompting with text and images to tune structure and reliability.

  1. State subject count and action first.
  2. Fix perspective, crop, and aspect ratio.
  3. Constrain what the reference should control, if any.

Troubleshooting: if the model invents extra objects or changes the count, include “one subject only,” “no crowd,” or “exclude additional people.” If faces skew, specify “neutral expression” or “eyes looking at camera.” When backgrounds get busy, add “clean backdrop” or “minimal street traffic.” Small, direct constraints curb failure modes without smothering creativity.

Control Style, Lighting, and Materials

Once the subject is grounded, steer the look with a compact stack of style modifiers. Think lenses, lighting, era, surface materials, and color system. A strong stack might read “cinematic 50mm, soft window light, 1970s palette, matte textures, shallow depth of field.” Each modifier should earn its place by affecting a different visual dimension. Avoid piling synonyms. Two or three well-chosen cues outperform a dozen conflicting ones. If you are matching production references, cite a lighting pattern like “Rembrandt lighting” or “butterfly lighting,” or a setup such as “golden hour backlight with fill card.”

Lighting words do heavy lifting. “Overcast diffuse” yields soft shadows and accurate color. “Hard noon sun” creates crisp edges and contrast. “Neon rim light” outlines subjects for nightlife scenes. For indoor scenes, add bounce direction with phrases like “soft key from camera left, gentle fill from right.” Materials matter too. Specify “brushed aluminum,” “glossy ceramic,” or “linen fabric” when texture carries brand meaning. In product scenes, call out surface treatments like “fingerprint-resistant,” which nudges the model away from oily specular hotspots.

Add Constraints, Negatives, and Brand Safety Checks

Constraints turn a good prompt into a dependable one. Use negative prompts to exclude unwanted motifs such as “no text, no watermark, no extra limbs, no logos, no brand marks.” Add technical guardrails if your tool supports them: “high detail but no skin pore exaggeration,” “balanced saturation,” or “avoid extreme distortion.” For Stable Diffusion style tools, keep a record of seed, sampler, and guidance scale. Seed locks randomness for reproducibility, sampler controls how images evolve, and guidance scale balances fidelity to your prompt versus exploration. Consistency is not an accident. It is logged parameters plus careful exclusions.

Brand safety requires more than negatives. Define a color range aligned to your style guide, list approved props, and set hard boundaries around sensitive contexts. If you are producing images for regulated categories like healthcare or finance, bake compliance into the prompt: “avoid depicting clinical procedures,” “no currency or financial statements in frame,” or “use generically labeled packaging.” Many teams maintain a shared bank of negative phrases that reflect brand and legal policy. Keep this list short and targeted. Over-long negatives can make models ignore important positive cues.

Before shipping, run a quick triage. Check for off-brand hues, incorrect demographics, or unintended symbolism. If a scene strays, adjust the smallest piece first. Tighten the subject action, clarify the prop list, or move one lighting cue. For multi-channel campaigns, test a small batch at each aspect ratio and compare. If you need a broader governance approach and test procedures, the comprehensive prompt engineering playbook covering patterns, templates, testing, and governance for text and image models offers patterns and scorecards you can adapt to your review cycle.

  1. List three to five negatives tied to brand policy.
  2. Record seed and sampler for reproducibility when available.
  3. Audit color, props, and context against your style guide.

Troubleshooting: if negatives flatten the image, remove the least important one and strengthen a positive cue instead. When outputs look noisy, raise guidance or simplify your style stack. If faces or hands are unreliable, generate at larger resolution or apply a targeted pass with a face restoration tool, then retrain your preset to include that step.

Test, Iterate, and Document

Treat your first pass as a lab test, not a final. Generate a grid that isolates one variable at a time. You might test lens cues first with the same subject and lighting, then swap lighting patterns while keeping lens fixed. Label each image with the exact prompt segment it represents. This small discipline speeds decision making and reveals which words move the needle. Teams at agencies often keep side-by-side boards of winning snippets that transfer across clients because the behaviors are stable across model versions.

Once you can reproduce a look reliably, package it as a prompt template with blanks for subject and action. For example: “{subject and count} performing {action} at {location}, {framing and lens}, {lighting pattern}, {material and palette}, aspect ratio {AR}, negatives {policy list}.” Store five to ten such templates for your main use cases: product hero, lifestyle portrait, flat lay, environmental scene, and detail macro. Templates reduce creative drift when deadlines compress and teams change. For brand campaigns that demand strict continuity, version your template and lock it for the project duration.

Document results and push them forward. Capture the prompt, seed, and settings that produced your favorite images, then attach notes on what to change when moving platforms. If you are mixing text and reference images, align your evidence with a clear structure for text-image prompting that prioritizes reliability so teams can swap tools without losing control. When you are ready to scale, draw from a wider library of campaign-ready templates with consistent framing to seed new ad sets quickly while protecting brand cues.

  1. Test one variable per grid.
  2. Promote winning stacks into reusable templates.
  3. Log seeds, settings, and change notes.

For governance and team onboarding, align your process with a playbook that covers evaluation, templates, and escalation paths. Strong documentation helps when model updates shift behavior. You can then diagnose and re-stabilize styles quickly.

Well-written prompts are production tools. You now have a method to define subjects, control style and light, set constraints, and test until results are steady. As models gain new controls for geometry, materials, and multi-image composition, fold them into your phases rather than starting from scratch. The result is a living system that keeps pace with change while preserving your brand’s visual voice.
