How Bidirectional EV Charging Works

By Leena Narayanan • August 25, 2025

How does bidirectional charging work? In short, the charger and vehicle coordinate to reverse power flow so the battery can push energy outward to a home, building, or grid. Under the hood, power electronics and control protocols convert DC to AC, regulate safety, and align dispatch with tariffs and reliability needs.

Below, we move from the physics in the cables to the orchestration in the cloud, pausing on practical tradeoffs and projects that show what runs reliably at scale.

Power flow, converters, and protection: the street-level mechanics

At its core, bidirectional charging flips the typical path: instead of AC from the grid becoming DC for the battery, stored DC is inverted back to AC for a load or feeder. This conversion can occur in the vehicle using an onboard inverter or in the charger using an offboard DC converter. The vehicle’s battery management system defines voltage and current limits, while the inverter modulates switching to produce a grid-synchronous waveform. Power factor control, active filtering, and phase balancing ensure that exported power is clean and aligned with local grid conditions.

Two main designs show up in the field. Onboard bidirectional systems, such as those tested with the Nissan LEAF in Denmark and the UK, integrate the inverter within the car, allowing smaller, lighter external equipment but placing thermal and durability requirements on the vehicle hardware. Offboard designs concentrate conversion in the charger cabinet, common in depot-scale DC units where shared power stages and better cooling improve uptime. Each topology must meet anti-islanding, ride-through, and harmonic distortion limits, often verified to UL 1741 SB in North America and through European norms aligned with ENTSO-E guidance.

Protection and interconnection are the guardrails that make V2X reliable. Grid-tied operation typically follows IEEE 1547 capabilities for distributed energy resources, including voltage and frequency ride-through and reactive power support. In practice, that means a bidirectional charger can hold voltage with volt-var curves and help stabilize frequency by adjusting watts within milliseconds. For premises integration, transfer switches and relays segregate V2H backup from the utility during outages, preventing unsafe backfeed. Metering is equally crucial. Separate channels or certified submetering distinguish charging consumption from export, enabling time-of-use optimization and compensation. Controls, protocols, and use cases: the system-wide choreography

Bidirectional charging depends on layered communication. At the plug, ISO 15118-20 defines high-level messages for energy transfer, certificates, and vehicle energy profiles, enabling features like Plug and Charge and negotiated discharge limits. Between the charger and the management platform, OCPP 2.0.1 carries configuration, metering, and smart charging commands. For utility orchestration, IEEE 2030.5 and OpenADR often deliver DER schedules and telemetry. Together, these protocols provide a contract: the vehicle advertises availability, the charger enforces safety, and the grid actor dispatches within agreed constraints. Real projects showcase the operational playbook. In Denmark, Nuvve and fleet partners demonstrated frequency regulation with Nissan LEAF vehicles, earning market revenue by tightly tracking grid signals. In the United States, Dominion Energy’s school bus initiative in Virginia turned idle summer capacity into grid support while cutting fleet fuel and maintenance costs. Japan’s disaster resilience planning frequently pairs CHAdeMO-equipped vehicles with public facilities for emergency power, a model that informed municipal drills after major storms. These deployments succeed when schedules respect mobility first. State of charge floors, driver departure times, and battery temperature windows are enforced locally by the charger and centrally by the aggregator to keep vehicles mission-ready.

For planners, the operational details matter as much as the physics. Depot operators blend time-of-use rates with demand charge management, often stacking V2G on top of smart charging to avoid coincident peaks. Building managers integrate chargers into energy management systems to provide demand response and ride-through support. Utilities craft tariffs and interconnection pathways that reward flexibility while protecting feeders.
