OCPP vs ISO 15118: Plug-and-Charge and Smart Control

By Leena Narayanan • August 25, 2025

OCPP and ISO 15118 are often mentioned in the same breath, yet they solve different layers of the EV charging puzzle. One orchestrates stations and networks, the other gives the vehicle a voice. Knowing where each applies helps you plan for Plug and Charge, smart charging, and eventual vehicle-to-grid without costly rework.

Below, we compare their scopes, security models, and operational maturity, then map them to practical procurement and rollout decisions for cities, fleets, and utilities.

OCPP vs ISO 15118: what they are and why it matters

Open Charge Point Protocol, or OCPP, is the lingua franca between an EV charging station and a central management system. Think of it as the remote control for a network operator. It provisions firmware, collects sessions, executes smart charging profiles, and handles diagnostics. Most public and depot networks run on OCPP 1.6J today, while OCPP 2.0.1 is emerging with richer device management, improved security profiles, and support for certificate handling to complement Plug and Charge workflows. The protocol is transport agnostic, commonly using WebSockets over TLS, and is maintained by the Open Charge Alliance with global certification programs.

ISO 15118 lives on the EV-to-EVSE link, primarily over PLC on the Combined Charging System. It standardizes how the car and charger negotiate charging parameters, authenticate identities, and support Plug and Charge. ISO 15118-2 covers foundational messaging for AC and DC charging and enables contract-based charging via certificates. ISO 15118-20, finalized more recently, expands to advanced energy management and formalizes bidirectional features that future V2G-capable vehicles and chargers can use. This standard integrates with a public key infrastructure so cars can authenticate without RFID cards or apps.

Why the comparison matters: OCPP and ISO 15118 are complementary, not interchangeable. A charging network can run OCPP without ISO 15118 by relying on RFID or app-based authentication. It can also support ISO 15118 for seamless vehicle identity while still needing OCPP to supervise the station fleet, push tariffs, and coordinate load across sites. Cities and utilities planning V2G must align both layers so ISO 15118-20 capabilities in the vehicle handshake propagate into grid-aware controls exposed via OCPP 2.0.1 and higher.

Plug and Charge, security, smart charging, and V2G readiness

Plug and Charge is the most visible differentiator. ISO 15118 defines the contract-based authentication using certificates that live in the vehicle and are validated by the charging ecosystem. It eliminates manual steps and reduces session errors. OCPP complements this by transporting certificate installation and revocation workflows between the station and central system in version 2.0.1, enabling operators to manage trust chains at scale. In practice, early deployments in Europe and North America show that interoperability hinges on aligned certificate policies and backend support, often coordinated through industry groups like CharIN and national PKI operators.

Security spans both layers. ISO 15118 specifies TLS on the EV link with mutual authentication options and strict message signing. OCPP recommends TLS for transport and provides security profiles that improve station identity, key management, and event logging. Operators should plan for lifecycle processes such as rotating keys, revoking compromised certificates, and maintaining audit trails that connect EV-facing events with backend records. This becomes critical when supporting payment-backed Plug and Charge and when meeting utility cybersecurity requirements tied to critical infrastructure classifications.

Smart charging is where OCPP shines today. Networks use OCPP 1.6J profiles to shape load by station, site, or group, integrating with tariffs and demand limits. OCPP 2.0.1 adds finer device modeling, meter qualifications, and event-driven control. ISO 15118-20 brings richer setpoints inside the EV session, including support for bidirectional power and scheduled flexibility, but widespread vehicle and charger support is still ramping. Cities like Singapore under the Smart Nation program and European urban pilots tied to AFIR preparations are testing coordinated load control that blends OCPP fleet commands with ISO 15118 vehicle negotiation to reduce peak demand and improve reliability.

Vehicle-to-grid readiness requires careful timing. ISO 15118-20 defines the vehicle handshake for bidirectional energy transfer, while OCPP provides the operational backbone to meter, settle, and orchestrate aggregated services. Many utilities are trialing V2G with fleets in depots where equipment can be tightly controlled and firmware aligned. For broader interoperability, align your station procurement to OCPP 2.0.1 and ISO 15118-20 roadmaps, confirm certification listings, and verify PKI compatibility. Procurement, migration paths, and who should choose what

For public networks and municipalities, prioritize OCPP 2.0.1 readiness in new chargers and require certification where available. This protects your ability to switch backends or operate mixed-vendor fleets. Add ISO 15118-2 today for Plug and Charge in regions where PKI programs are established. As vehicle support for ISO 15118-20 grows, plan field upgrades that enable bidirectional features first at controlled sites such as depots and campuses. Barcelona’s ongoing smart city work and Nordic pilots demonstrate that staged rollouts with clear interoperability test plans reduce downtime and help operators validate roaming, tariffs, and fault handling before scaling.

For fleets and depots, load management and reliability come first. Choose chargers with OCPP 1.6J or 2.0.1 smart charging profiles and proven integration with your energy management system. Add ISO 15118 mainly where Plug and Charge shortens turnaround times or reduces badge management overhead. When exploring V2G for peak shaving or resilience, ensure metering accuracy and protection coordination meet utility interconnection requirements. Many fleet operators pair site controls with actionable practices like dynamic demand limits and managed charging blocks; see these strategies for managing EV charging load across depots, fleets, and buildings for an implementation-oriented checklist.

For utilities and aggregators, interoperability and cybersecurity drive value. Align station onboarding with approved PKI roots and certificate policies, and require event logs that correlate ISO 15118 session IDs with OCPP transaction records for settlement and auditing. Where distribution operators use IEEE 2030.5 or OpenADR for DER coordination, confirm your charging backend can translate site-level constraints into OCPP profiles while preserving EV-level protections negotiated under ISO 15118. If you are in the early planning phase, this V2G standards explainer covering OCPP, ISO 15118, IEEE 2030.5, UL, and grid codes maps control pathways and regulatory touchpoints that affect timelines and cost.

Migrating without disruption requires governance. Specify firmware upgrade paths, schema versions, and fallback behaviors in contracts. Adopt conformance and plugfest testing where available, and budget for pilot phases that include roaming partners. Cities participating in EU AFIR preparations are demonstrating phased enablement: start with OCPP-conformant smart charging and session transparency, add ISO 15118 Plug and Charge at priority sites with strong customer adoption, then target -20 bidirectional features once both vehicles and submetering are certified. The result is a network that evolves without stranding assets.

Verdict: use OCPP as the backbone for fleet control, data, and grid coordination, and deploy ISO 15118 to streamline driver experience and enable future bidirectional services. If your primary goal is seamless authentication with minimal friction, prioritize ISO 15118-2 with a well-governed PKI. If your near-term objective is cost-effective load shaping across many vendors, lean on OCPP 1.6J or 2.0.1 today and phase in ISO 15118-20 as vehicles catch up. The best outcomes come from treating them as complementary layers that converge toward resilient, grid-aware charging.
