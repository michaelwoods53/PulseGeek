How to Set Dimming Schedules for Smart Street Lights

By Leena Narayanan • Published October 18, 2025

Setting dimming schedules for smart street lights is how cities align savings with safety without guesswork. This guide defines prerequisites, shows how to structure schedules, and outlines verification so changes are auditable and reversible. We assume a connected lighting system with controllable drivers, access to device logs, and basic field support for spot checks.

Key takeaways

  • Start from policy and road class to bound dimming limits.
  • Use astronomical triggers with offsets to handle seasonal change.
  • Layer motion boosts carefully and cap maximum boost duration.
  • Validate with logs plus lux or current readings at sample poles.
  • Stage rollouts and keep a rollback schedule ready to apply.

Plan the work

Begin with a clear policy envelope so dimming schedules remain defensible and predictable. Define minimum light levels by road class, such as higher baselines on pedestrian corridors and lower levels on service lanes, then note exceptions like crosswalks or transit stops. A practical range is a 30 to 60 percent reduction during low activity, with safeguards near intersections and schools. The tradeoff is energy savings versus perceived safety, which shifts by land use and hour. Lock these rules into a one page brief so later decisions map back to intent. Doing so prevents ad hoc overrides that creep over time and makes your schedule audit friendly.

Translate goals into measurable outcomes that dimming schedules can actually influence. Examples include percentage energy reduction against a pre retrofit baseline, compliance with curfew lighting requirements, and target call volume for light related complaints. Pick two or three metrics and define how you will measure them, such as driver current from node telemetry and ticket categories from the service desk. Beware vanity metrics like total hours dimmed that ignore where reductions happened. Tying each chosen measure to a data source ensures you can verify improvements and justify adjustments later without speculation.

Segment the network so schedules reflect patterns on the ground rather than a one size approach. Group by road class, land use, and activity windows, for example downtown main street, residential side streets, and parks with evening events. Add tags for sensitive zones like crosswalk approaches that should hold higher minimums. The risk in coarse segmentation is uneven visibility where it matters most, while overly granular groups complicate operations and testing. Aim for three to six schedule groups to start. This balance keeps configuration manageable, supports phased rollouts, and lets you compare results between segments without noise.

Prepare environment

Confirm device capabilities so your dimming schedules map to what hardware can do. Check driver dimming range, supported control signals like 0 to 10 V or DALI, and whether nodes support astronomical triggers or only fixed times. If the system lacks accurate time sync, add a gateway or verify NTP reachability to avoid drift. Edge cases include mixed driver models on one circuit, which can cap achievable minimums. Capture these constraints in a short inventory, because later validation depends on knowing which poles should behave identically and which should not due to physical limits.

Gather reliable inputs that will anchor schedule timing and boosts. Obtain sunrise and sunset data for your latitude, ideally through a library using solar geometry, and define offsets to smooth transitions. Collect occupancy insights from traffic counts, motion sensors, or camera free analytics if available, then translate them into boost rules like 20 percent for up to five minutes when motion is detected. Beware noisy sensors that trigger frequently in windy conditions. By preparing sources and rules now, you reduce guesswork during configuration and prevent patterns that oscillate or create strobe like changes that can annoy residents.

Set up roles and access so schedule changes are controlled and recoverable. Create a staging area or test group of five to ten poles that mirror the main segments, and ensure you can push schedules to that group without touching production zones. Enable audit logging and export, so edits record who changed what and when. A simple rollback file per group provides insurance against misconfiguration. The tradeoff is extra setup time now for faster recovery later, but that exchange pays off the first time a schedule misfires during a storm or a holiday event and you need to revert quickly.

Execute steps

Use a simple, repeatable structure to define dimming schedules so you can reason about the outcome. A reliable pattern is a daily timeline with evening ramp, late night low, pre dawn lift, and morning off. Each block defines a target level and a guardrail such as minimum and maximum. Add astronomical events with offsets to avoid abrupt changes at sunset and sunrise, and reserve occupancy boosts that overlay the baseline without exceeding a safe cap. The benefit is composable behavior that is easy to explain and test. The limitation is rare events like fog, which may require temporary overrides outside the normal rhythm.

Before pushing to production groups, encode one schedule in a compact format that your platform accepts. JSON works well as a neutral example and can be translated into vendor specific templates. Include fields for group name, baseline blocks with start and level, astronomical offsets, and motion boosts with dwell and cap. The goal is readability that a reviewer can audit quickly. Avoid fancy expressions that hide timing logic inside nested conditions. Once encoded, you can run a dry run by applying it to a test group and comparing node telemetry for expected levels at key times like one hour after midnight and thirty minutes before sunrise.

Validate results

Verify that dimming schedules behave as designed using both data and field checks. Start with gateway or node logs that show commanded levels and actual driver currents at defined times, such as thirty minutes after your late night change and ten minutes before sunrise. If the platform exposes exceptions like missed commands, record counts per group. Then sample three to five poles per segment with a light meter or a trusted current reading to confirm the baseline matches expectations within a reasonable tolerance. This blended approach catches logic errors and physical issues like a failed driver that would otherwise skew averages.

Confirm astronomical timing and offsets use the correct timezone and latitude so transitions occur as expected. A quick cross check is to plot one week of on and off times against a trusted sunrise and sunset source for your city and ensure the offset is applied consistently. Edge cases include daylight saving transitions, where the schedule may shift an hour if time sync lags. If your platform allows a simulated date replay, use it to preview behavior across those boundary days. Doing so prevents jarring changes that might cause complaints on the first Monday after a seasonal clock change.

Evaluate occupancy boosts for stability and usefulness rather than sheer activity. Review motion event rates per hour and overlay them on a graph of commanded levels to see whether boosts cluster or spread. Excessive stacking indicates noisy sensors, while sparse boosts might justify a small increase in dwell to cover typical crossing times. Run an A and B comparison across two similar blocks for a week with different dwell settings and compare ticket counts or patrol feedback. The goal is a boost that feels responsive without turning side streets into near daytime light during quiet hours.

Troubleshoot and optimize

Address schedule drift first by confirming time synchronization on gateways and nodes. If drift appears in logs, verify NTP reachability and ensure devices inherit the correct timezone, then reapply schedules after time correction. For isolated poles that ignore commands, check signal quality or power cycling events that interrupt execution. A tradeoff emerges when increasing retry windows, which can reduce misses but delay updates. Keep a rollback file per group and apply it if behavior remains inconsistent after clock fixes, since reverting to a known good baseline stabilizes the system while you investigate the root cause.

Resolve resident complaints by mapping them to schedule blocks and locations. If reports concentrate near crosswalks or transit stops, raise the safety minimum or extend the pre dawn lift on adjacent poles. When calls mention glare, review boost caps and ensure they do not exceed comfortable levels compared to the evening baseline. Watch for unintended consequences like bright patches that make nearby darker areas feel less safe. Small, localized edits applied during off peak hours reduce disruption. After changes, follow up with a short monitoring window and document the adjustment so future audits track the reasoning and effect.

Refine efficiency with data informed experiments once stability is proven. Try lowering the late night baseline by five percent for one week in residential segments and compare energy draw and complaint rates to the prior period. Introduce modest motion dwell increases where crossing times are longer, such as near parks, while keeping cooldowns short to avoid stacking. The risk is trial fatigue if too many variables change at once, so alter only one parameter per group per week. This cadence builds confidence, teaches which levers matter, and keeps dimming schedules aligned with evolving patterns without causing confusion.

Quick start checklist

  1. Set policy bounds: write minimum levels and exceptions by road class.
  2. Create segments: group poles by land use and sensitive locations.
  3. Draft schedule: define baseline blocks with astronomical offsets.
  4. Add boosts: set dwell, cooldown, and a conservative max cap.
  5. Test on staging: apply to sample poles and review telemetry.
  6. Roll out safely: deploy by segment and keep a rollback file.

Frequently asked questions

How low can I safely dim residential streets at night?

A common safe floor is around 30 to 40 percent, with higher minimums near crosswalks, schools, and transit stops. Start conservatively, validate with field checks and complaints, and adjust in small steps per segment.

Should I use fixed times or astronomical triggers?

Astronomical triggers with offsets adapt to seasons without manual edits. Fixed times can work for predictable zones but risk early darkness or bright dawn in winter or summer. Use offsets to smooth transitions at sunset and sunrise.

How do I prevent motion boosts from stacking endlessly?

Set a cooldown between boosts, cap the maximum boosted level, and bound the dwell duration. If sensors are noisy, increase debounce or sensitivity thresholds. Review event rates per hour to confirm the changes reduced stacking.

What if schedules shift an hour after a time change?

Check device time synchronization and timezone settings, then correct clocks and reapply schedules. Review a week of transitions across the daylight saving boundary and confirm offsets align with local sunrise and sunset times.

How do I roll back quickly if complaints spike?

Maintain a known good schedule file per segment. If issues arise, apply the rollback during off peak hours, monitor key locations, and document the event. This stabilizes service while you analyze logs and refine settings.

Looking ahead

Treat dimming schedules as living policy that adapts with seasons, land use, and community feedback. Set a quarterly review to compare energy draw, complaint patterns, and patrol notes against your original goals, then prioritize one or two targeted adjustments per segment.
