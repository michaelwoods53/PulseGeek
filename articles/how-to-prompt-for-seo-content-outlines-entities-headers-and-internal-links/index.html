Prompt for SEO Outlines: Entities, Headers, Internal Links

By Evie Rao • August 27, 2025

When you ask a model for an SEO outline, you are not only organizing ideas. You are encoding a map of entities, user tasks, and internal paths through your site. The right prompts turn a vague topic into a structured draft that aligns with search intent and reduces rewrites.

This guide shows how to prompt for SEO content outlines with entities, headers, and internal links that search engines and readers can follow. We will move from discovery to structure, then finish with quality checks and refinements.

Discover entities and intent before drafting

Begin by anchoring your outline to real entities and intent types. Entities are people, places, organizations, products, and concepts that disambiguate meaning. When your prompt names the core and related entities, language models tend to stay on-topic and avoid generic filler. Google’s public guidance on helpful content prioritizes specificity and first-hand expertise, which entities help you encode from the start.

Use a lightweight research loop to gather candidates. Look at the top results for your target query, skim the “People also ask” questions, and scan Wikipedia or product specs for canonical names. Map intent to a primary outcome such as informational how-to, comparison, or transactional readiness. This step mirrors the practical approach taught in a comprehensive prompt engineering playbook covering patterns, templates, testing, and governance, where constraints and evaluation criteria reduce drift.

Now translate the research into an input block the model can use. Include the primary query, intent label, must-cover entities, and user tasks. List exclusions to avoid misleading overlaps. If you need consistent tone and audience alignment, incorporate persona cues using persona-based prompts for marketing with reusable templates, tone controls, and SEO-ready structures. This gives the model context for level of detail and examples.

  1. Collect entities: primary topic, brand or standards bodies, adjacent concepts, and conflicting or ambiguous terms to avoid.

  2. Label intent: informational, comparison, or transactional and state the reader’s job to be done in one sentence.

  3. Specify audience and voice constraints so examples, metrics, and vocabulary match expectations.

Tip: If the topic risks medical, legal, or financial YMYL concerns, add a safety line requesting source-backed statements and defer to authoritative guidelines. This reduces the chance of the model introducing unfounded claims.

Structure H2/H3 headers around entity clusters

With entities and intent in hand, design headers that form a logical journey. Each H2 should resolve a distinct reader task and each H3 should clarify sub-decisions or steps. Reinforce coverage by placing entities where they naturally belong rather than stuffing them. Google’s Search Central documents emphasize clear headings that reflect the content’s structure and not just keywords.

Prompt the model to produce a hierarchical outline with token budgets and coverage notes. Ask for H2 purpose statements and bullet-level acceptance criteria for each H3. Include a checklist to prevent common gaps such as prerequisites, edge cases, and alternatives. This mirrors testable specification writing, which makes outlines easier to review and expand.

Here is a compact pattern you can adapt. Replace placeholders with your research and keep the acceptance criteria concrete so another writer could execute without guessing.

  1. Directive: “Draft an outline for [query], intent [label], audience [persona]. Use 3–5 H2s, each with 2–4 H3s.”

  2. Entities: “Must naturally cover [list]. Avoid conflation with [list].”

  3. Coverage notes: “For each H2, provide a one-sentence purpose and H3 acceptance criteria. Include examples and tradeoffs.”

  4. Quality gates: “No unsupported claims. Cite standard definitions where relevant. Keep headings descriptive, not clickbait.”

Troubleshooting: If outputs become generic, tighten the acceptance criteria and cap H2 count to force prioritization. If the outline bloats, require a word budget per H2 and ask the model to remove low-signal sections. If a topic has regional nuances, request a region-specific variant outline and highlight where regulations or terminology differ.

Weave internal links and run a quality pass

Internal links give readers the next best page for their task and help search engines understand relationships. Treat them as part of the outline, not an afterthought. Identify 2–4 destination pages that deepen key sections, and write the anchor text as if it appeared naturally in a body paragraph. Avoid anchors that repeat page titles or exact keywords.

In your prompt, include a short catalog of candidate pages with their purpose and ideal anchor themes. For this topic, you might link from an H2 about prompt structure to the site’s broader methodology page and from a section on tone to a resource with persona-led voice controls. Provide the exact slugs so the model can format consistent links later.

Use this insertion pattern to guide the model during outline generation and reduce later editing.

  1. Context list: “Available pages include [slug] for [purpose], and [slug] for [purpose].”

  2. Anchors: “Compose natural anchors that reflect the destination’s summary, not its title.”

  3. Placement: “Suggest insertion points under relevant H2/H3s with 1-sentence rationale.”

Quality assurance matters. Run a quick check: are all must-cover entities placed where readers need them, do the H2s ladder up to intent, and do the internal links create useful paths without cannibalizing a target page? If you manage multiple languages, generate outlines per locale first, then internationalize links carefully to avoid sending readers to the wrong market page. As a final step, evaluate your draft against a lightweight rubric inspired by rigorous patterns in a playbook that stresses testing and governance for text models. This practice tightens consistency release to release.

Ready to take the next step? If your team relies on brand voices or segments, align outline prompts with persona cues and tone controls supported by reusable marketing templates so coverage and style stay coherent across articles.

Apply the pattern, measure outcomes, and iterate

After you generate outlines with entities, headers, and links, run a small pilot before scaling. Choose a low-risk topic, publish two versions from the same outline with different internal link placements, and observe engagement. Track scroll depth to see whether H2 order supports user intent, then reorder if readers stall early. This mirrors how many teams approached site improvements following Google’s helpful-content updates by prioritizing clarity and usefulness over keyword volume.

Document what worked in your prompt. Keep snippets for entity blocks, acceptance criteria, and link catalogs so other writers can reuse them. When taxonomy changes or you add a new product line, update the entity list and internal link targets across templates. Treat your outline prompts as living, governed assets rather than ad hoc messages in a chat window.

Finally, create a feedback loop. Ask editors and subject experts to rate coverage and accuracy, and log recurring gaps. Every few weeks, tighten the constraints and refresh examples to reflect new standards or features. If you introduce structured data or new content types, include those requirements in your acceptance criteria so outlines evolve in step with your site architecture.

With a disciplined pattern and clear QA gates, your prompts will consistently produce SEO outlines that respect entities, guide readers with purposeful headers, and connect content with helpful internal links. The result is a drafting process that scales without sacrificing quality and a library of prompts that improves with each release.
