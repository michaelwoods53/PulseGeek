Mempool Mechanics: Fees, Priority, and Propagation

By Maya Navarre • Published October 10, 2025

The mempool is where valid transactions wait before miners include them in a block. Understanding fees, priority, and propagation helps predict confirmation time and reduce surprises during congestion. We will follow a transaction from broadcast through node validation, show how feerates drive ranking, and study propagation patterns that affect who sees your transaction and when. Along the way, we will contrast wallet tactics like replace-by-fee and child-pays-for-parent, and note how eviction policies change during load spikes. By the end, you will have a mental model that connects node behavior to user outcomes, with concrete steps to tune reliability without overspending.

Key takeaways

  • Feerate per vbyte, not absolute fee, sets transaction priority.
  • Propagation speed depends on validation cost and peer bandwidth.
  • RBF and CPFP can rescue stuck transactions with careful limits.
  • Nodes evict low feerate entries when mempool exceeds size limits.
  • Accurate fee estimates require recent mempool and block history.

From broadcast to mempool: how transactions find their place

Nodes accept a transaction into the mempool only after policy checks and script validation pass without hitting resource limits. A clear rule of thumb is that fully verified inputs, standard scripts, and non-dust outputs are required for admission. For example, a transaction with oversized witness data might still be valid by consensus yet rejected by default policy to protect node bandwidth. This gap between consensus and policy is intentional and reduces abuse during flood attempts. The tradeoff is that some edge cases that could confirm remain unseen by many peers, slowing propagation. Understanding that mempool entry is policy-scoped explains why a transaction may propagate quickly on some nodes while others refuse it.

Once admitted, the transaction propagates across peers using an inventory advertisement and request pattern that prioritizes compact announcements. Peers first share short identifiers or sketches, then request full data only for unknown or updated transactions. This two-stage exchange reduces bandwidth during heavy activity and helps nodes avoid duplicate downloads. The side effect is that first-hop peers and their relay preferences influence who sees your transaction soonest. During bursts, nodes may stagger announcements to limit CPU spikes, which slows propagation but keeps validation responsive. Wallets that broadcast through multiple outbound peers often achieve faster coverage because they bypass any single peer bottleneck or temporary relay filter.

Local mempools represent each node’s current view, not a canonical global queue, which explains timing divergences. For instance, a node with a smaller mempool size limit will evict low feerate entries sooner, changing which transactions it relays onward. A peer that has recently restarted might have a sparse mempool and request more data, increasing your transaction’s spread from that vantage point. Conversely, peers behind slow links may drop announcements during backpressure and only fetch high-priority items. The practical takeaway is that propagation speed varies with peer topology, policy settings, and transient load, so confirmation predictions should include uncertainty bands rather than a single estimate.

Section highlights

  • Mempool entry depends on node policy, not only consensus validity.
  • Expect variable propagation due to topology, limits, and load.

Fee markets and priority: who gets mined first

Priority in modern networks is driven by feerate, commonly measured in sats per virtual byte, rather than the absolute fee amount. Miners and nodes assemble blocks by selecting the highest feerate transactions while respecting dependencies, which means small but dense transactions often win over large low-rate ones. A workable baseline is to choose a feerate that landed within the last few blocks during similar congestion. The catch is that blocks vary in composition and available space, so short windows can mislead. During spikes, wallets should consider replace-by-fee for flexibility, but only after ensuring recipients accept it, since some flows rely on non-replaceable commits for safety.

Dependency chains matter because ancestors must be included before or with their descendants, which is why child-pays-for-parent can rescue a stuck low-fee parent. The idea is to attach a high-fee child that lifts the package feerate above competing entries. A concrete scenario is sweeping a UTXO with a low-rate parent by spending its output in a high-rate child, raising the combined incentive. The risk is overpayment if conditions ease, and some nodes enforce package limits that can cap the rescue strategy. As a rule, test a small variant first when time is flexible, and escalate only if the mempool remains competitive for several blocks.

When setting fees programmatically, query recent confirmation targets and mempool statistics from a local node to avoid stale third-party data. The expected outcome is a feerate range aligned with your time target plus guardrails for volatility. Propagation speed and user outcomes

Faster propagation increases the chance that miners include your transaction because more templates see it before block assembly. A practical technique is to broadcast through multiple well-connected peers and wait for a propagation threshold before assuming visibility. Some wallets track how many distinct peers echoed the transaction’s inventory message, then adjust fee bumping if spread stalls. The limitation is that echo counts can misrepresent miner connectivity, so treat them as signals rather than guarantees. For critical transfers, consider time-locked splits or batching strategies that reduce size and raise feerate efficiency, while watching mempool churn that can demote your position during sudden fee spikes.

Verification efficiency also shapes propagation, since low-cost validation lets nodes relay more transactions per second during stress. Cryptographic scaffolding like Merkle roots in block headers enables compact proofs that wallets can verify quickly without downloading entire blocks. This matters when checking whether your transaction was actually mined or remains pending. Start with node-sourced estimates, observe early propagation, and keep RBF or CPFP as measured follow-ups instead of reflexes. Key terms

  • Entropy — Entropy measures uncertainty in data. Systems need strong entropy to generate keys, seeds, and random numbers that attackers cannot predict or reproduce.
  • Mempool — The mempool holds unconfirmed transactions waiting to be added to a block. It affects ordering, fees, and attack surfaces like front-running and MEV.

Frequently asked questions

Why is feerate more important than absolute fee?

Miners maximize revenue per block, so they prefer transactions that pay more per virtual byte. A small but dense transaction can outrank a larger one with a higher total fee if the larger one pays a lower feerate. This ordering drives selection.

How long should I wait before using replace by fee?

Wait a few blocks at your target confirmation window unless there is a sudden feerate spike. If the mempool is growing and peers are not relaying your transaction widely, consider a modest bump. Avoid aggressive increases that overshoot the market.

Can child pays for parent always unstuck a transaction?

No. Package limits and policy filters can block an otherwise valid child. The combined package feerate must exceed competing entries, and some nodes limit ancestor count or size. Use CPFP when you control the child and fees justify the uplift.

Why do different explorers show different mempool sizes?

Each node maintains its own mempool with local policy and size limits. Connectivity and restart timing also change which transactions are seen. As a result, explorers that query different nodes often report different mempool sizes at the same time.

Does broadcasting to more peers always improve confirmation?

It usually increases early visibility, which helps. However, if your feerate is far below the active range, broad broadcast does little. Priority still depends on incentives, and backpressure on peers may throttle low-value announcements during congestion.
