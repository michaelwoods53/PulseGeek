Build Load Forecasting for DR Optimization

By Leena Narayanan • August 25, 2025

Accurate load forecasting is the keystone of demand response optimization. The steps below move from data foundations to operational playbooks so you can act on forecasts, not just admire them. We will keep one eye on practicality and the other on interoperability so your work scales with your smart grid roadmap.

Assemble and validate AMI data

Start where signal meets system: interval data from AMI meters. Pull at least 12 months of 15‑minute or hourly reads with meter metadata, service class, premise geolocation, and feeder or transformer identifiers. Add synchronized weather histories with dry‑bulb temperature, humidity, and solar irradiance, along with calendar features such as holidays and school schedules. If you run price‑based or event‑based programs, tag intervals with event windows, notification times, and incentive type to make later causality checks straightforward. Utilities in Singapore’s Smart Nation program and Barcelona’s smart city efforts demonstrate how consistent, granular data unlocks both neighborhood and feeder‑level insights, which will matter when you operationalize forecasts for targeted dispatch.

Quality gates pay off. Enforce strict timestamp validation, remove duplicated reads, and flag clock drift between AMI headend and weather sources. Implement rolling plausibility checks such as consumption thresholds by segment, day‑to‑day variance limits, and sudden level shifts that align with meter exchanges or premise moves. For missing spans, favor model‑based imputation that respects daily and weekly seasonality rather than flat fills. Partition your population into coherent cohorts, for example small commercial refrigeration, residential heat pumps, and office HVAC, since weather sensitivity and price elasticity vary widely across these segments.

Stand up a reproducible data pipeline before you touch modeling. A simple architecture includes ingestion, validation, feature engineering, and versioned training sets. Document transformations like temperature lags, cooling and heating degree hours, holiday proximity, and sunrise or sunset proxies. That ensures your load forecasting artifacts map cleanly to future device classes, firmware updates, and communication upgrades without costly rework.

Build and benchmark models

Choose models that reflect the action you will take. For day‑ahead portfolio planning, gradient boosted trees and generalized additive models offer reliable accuracy and interpretability. For intra‑day updates, lightweight regression with weather nowcasts and recent lags can outperform heavier neural networks because they retrain and deploy quickly. If you need feeder‑level visibility, consider hierarchical forecasting that jointly fits system, feeder, and segment levels, then reconciles forecasts so they add up. Keep uncertainty front and center. Quantile regression, prediction intervals from gradient boosting, or conformal methods give operators the range they need to size and time events with confidence.

Turn the modeling process into a controlled experiment. Split data by time to avoid leakage, holding out the most recent months for evaluation under realistic conditions. Measure mean absolute error for interpretability, but also track peak window accuracy, coverage of prediction intervals, and bias during heat waves or cold snaps. In cities like Barcelona, heat events cluster with tourism seasonality, so test on those periods explicitly. Build feature sets that capture behavior you can influence through demand response, such as thermostat setback responsiveness or pre‑cooling windows, rather than only global seasonality. Feature importance and partial dependence plots can reveal which signals meaningfully drive event timing.

Follow these steps to produce a DR‑ready forecast and avoid common pitfalls:

  1. Engineer actionable features: temperature lags, degree hours, prior‑day maximum, holiday distance, and program enrollment flags.
  2. Train multiple candidates: baseline regression, gradient boosting, and a simple LSTM for comparison. Keep hyperparameters modest for stability.
  3. Evaluate on event and near‑event windows. Record error, bias, and interval coverage in the 2–4 hours before system peaks.
  4. Select a champion and a lightweight challenger. Automate daily backtesting to catch drift and trigger retraining thresholds.
  5. Package outputs with quantiles and reason codes so operators see both the number and the narrative behind it.

Integrate forecasts with DR operations

Forecasts become valuable when they drive decisions. Export hourly and 15‑minute predictions with prediction intervals and feed them into your DRMS to size events, pick targeted feeders, and schedule notifications. Align identifiers with your AMI headend so device groups map cleanly to event payloads. Where possible, coordinate with distribution management systems to avoid over‑curtailing critical feeders. Many US utilities in wholesale markets schedule events using forecast‑driven playbooks that combine temperature triggers and margin thresholds. Build similar logic into your operations to select between pre‑cooling, short aggressive curtailments, or longer, moderate events based on uncertainty and customer segment mix.

Interoperability reduces friction as programs scale. Use a standards‑aware integration pattern so DRMS, AMI headend, and analytics exchange consistent data and events. Run operational playbooks and troubleshoot issues

Codify repeatable responses for high‑risk days. For heat waves, your playbook might pre‑cool residential HVAC cohorts two hours before the predicted peak, then stage shorter curtailments for commercial refrigeration to maintain product integrity. If the forecast interval widens beyond a threshold, allocate a larger share of controllable C&I loads and reduce reliance on price‑responsive residential segments. Incorporate learnings from known programs, such as how California utilities prioritize customer comfort and notification clarity during extreme events, to maintain participation and avoid attrition.

When forecasts drift, investigate systematically. Start with data freshness and schema changes from the AMI headend, then check weather station substitutions or sudden shifts in program enrollment. Use rolling backtests to detect under‑prediction during atypical holidays or school breaks. Maintain a simple decision tree for root causes: missing intervals, device firmware changes, altered business hours, or new DER behind the meter. If DER proliferation is accelerating in your territory, future‑proof your approach by tracking export‑capable meters separately and reconciling forecasts at the feeder level to capture net load effects.

Harden communications and customer touchpoints. If you publish time‑of‑use signals or event notices through the AMI network, align message cadence and payload size with device constraints to minimize delivery delays.
