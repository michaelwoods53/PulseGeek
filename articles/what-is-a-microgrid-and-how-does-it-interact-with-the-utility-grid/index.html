Microgrids and the Utility Grid: What to Know

By Leena Narayanan • August 23, 2025

What is a microgrid and how does it interact with the utility grid? In short, it is a local energy system that can run with the main grid or operate on its own, coordinating generation, storage, and loads to serve a defined area.

The interaction hinges on controlled exchange of power and data. With the right hardware and software, microgrids support reliability for both the site and the wider distribution system.

What is a microgrid?

A microgrid is a bounded electrical network that serves a campus, neighborhood, or facility with local generation, energy storage, and controllable loads. It has a clear point of common coupling that can open to island the system or close to synchronize with the utility. Typical assets include solar PV, batteries, flexible HVAC, and sometimes engines or fuel cells for extended outages. The controller continuously balances supply and demand, maintaining voltage and frequency within tight limits.

Two operating modes matter. In grid-connected mode, the microgrid exchanges power with the utility, using price and constraint signals to import, export, or hold neutral. In islanded mode, protection isolates the site after a disturbance, and a designated grid-forming resource sets voltage and frequency. This requires fast control and ride-through capability to keep critical loads online through transients and reclose events.

Real projects illustrate the pattern. UC San Diego’s long-running campus microgrid uses combined heat and power, solar, and storage to supply most of its demand while staying synchronized with the local utility for stability services. The Borrego Springs microgrid in California has islanded entire neighborhoods during wildfire-related outages, coordinating batteries and automation on the distribution feeder. Singapore’s Smart Nation initiatives include utility-scale trials of advanced controls and demand response that inform urban microgrid operations across dense districts.

How does a microgrid interact with the utility grid?

Interconnection starts with the point of common coupling, where relays, breakers, and anti-islanding logic protect both sides. Grid-tied operation depends on inverter-based resource settings like Volt/VAR and frequency-watt, which help manage voltage fluctuations and frequency excursions on the feeder. When bulk or local conditions tighten, the microgrid can curtail PV, dispatch batteries, or modulate flexible loads to maintain export limits and keep power quality within standards.

Coordination scales through software. Many utilities use ADMS and increasingly DERMS to model feeders, set operating envelopes, and send dispatch signals. Microgrids respond through hierarchical control that blends fast droop behavior with slower optimization stages. In practice, this looks like a site holding a prescribed export schedule while providing rapid voltage support during cloud transients. Projects such as the Santa Rita Jail microgrid in California have demonstrated seamless transitions through faults while maintaining critical operations and later resynchronizing once the grid is stable.

Value flows in both directions. When connected, microgrids can reduce peak loading, provide reactive power, and support black start procedures during restoration. During outages, they keep essential services running, which reduces stress on the wider system. Urban utilities are moving toward standardized, interoperable approaches so thousands of sites can participate safely. Control choices matter. Grid-following inverters are common, but grid-forming modes are rising to support stable islands and smooth resynchronization. If you want a deeper comparison of droop, hierarchical, and predictive approaches for grid-tied behavior, this survey of grid-tied microgrid control strategies outlines tradeoffs for stability and response speed.

Looking ahead, flexible market interfaces will let microgrids bid fast frequency response and non-wires alternatives while meeting local resilience targets. Teams preparing for that future benefit from disciplined settings, accurate feeder models, and clear roles between site controllers and utility platforms. If you are tuning inverters for grid support, consult proven practices for Volt/VAR and ride-through to avoid nuisance trips and maximize hosting headroom under variable conditions.
